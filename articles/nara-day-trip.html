Nara Beyond the Deer: What to Do After the First Hour

Yes, the deer are free-roaming and yes, they will bow to get crackers from you. This takes about 40 minutes. Nara has a full day's worth of things after that.

In this article

  1. The deer
  2. Todai-ji
  3. Kasuga Taisha
  4. Naramachi
  5. Getting there and timing

The deer

Nara Park has approximately 1,200 sika deer that have been designated national treasures and have, over generations of contact with tourists, learned that a particular bowing gesture triggers cracker-giving. You can buy shika-senbei (deer crackers) from vendors at ﾂ･200 a pack. The deer will absolutely approach you. Some will grab your bag. One will probably bite you lightly on the jacket. This is fine and expected and takes under an hour before it becomes routine. The question is what you do with the rest of the day.

Todai-ji

Todai-ji Temple: the largest wooden building in the world and home to a 15-meter bronze Buddha (the Nara Daibutsu) that has been sitting in approximately the same position since 752 AD. Entrance is ﾂ･600. The scale is genuinely difficult to convey 窶・the Buddha's nostril is the size of a small door, and there is a pillar inside the building with a hole at the base that's the same diameter as the nostril, which children (and optimistic adults) attempt to crawl through as a matter of ritual. Budget 45 minutes.

Kasuga Taisha

Kasuga Taisha: a Shinto shrine established in 768 AD, famous for its 3,000 stone and bronze lanterns that are lit twice a year (Setsubun in February and Obon in August). The path through the cedar forest to reach it is as good as the shrine itself 窶・moss-covered stone lanterns lining the approach, deer wandering between the cedars. Entry to the outer grounds is free; the inner precinct is ﾂ･500.

Naramachi

Naramachi: the preserved merchant town directly south of Nara Park. Narrow alleys, machiya townhouses, textile shops, sake breweries, and craft cafﾃｩs 窶・the kind of neighborhood that was here 200 years ago and has been left largely intact. It's quieter than the park area and full of places to eat. Nakatanidou mochi shop has a famous kinako mochi (pounded rice cake) that queues extend to the street; the Kasugayama area has older restaurants with proper set lunch menus.

Getting there and timing

Nara is 45 minutes from Kyoto by JR (ﾂ･730 or JR Pass) or about the same on the Kintetsu Line. It works as a half-day or full-day trip from either Kyoto or Osaka. The main park and Todai-ji can be crowded in the morning; the deer and crowds thin significantly after 3pm. If you're coming on a weekday and can arrive at 8:30am, the park before the day-trippers show up is a genuinely different experience.
