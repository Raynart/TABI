Day Trips From Tokyo That Aren't Hakone or Nikko

Hakone is excellent. Nikko is impressive. Both are in every guidebook and overrun on weekends. There are better options if you're willing to look slightly further.

The problem with the usual recommendations

The standard day-trip recommendations from Tokyo are Hakone (hot springs, Fuji views), Nikko (elaborate Edo-period shrines in the mountains), and Kamakura (giant Buddha, seaside). All three are worth visiting. All three are also predictable, and in peak season they're handling tourist numbers that test their infrastructure. If you've already been to any of them, or if you're planning a second Tokyo visit and want to branch out, there are options that deliver comparable experiences with significantly smaller crowds.

Kawagoe

Kawagoe, 30 minutes from Ikebukuro on the Tobu Tojo Line (around ﾂ･470). Called Little Edo, it has a preserved merchant district (Kurazukuri Street) with Edo-period clay-walled warehouses that have become antique shops, sweet shops, and cafﾃｩs. The bell tower that rings every six hours has been doing so since 1624. The Kashiya Yokocho (Candy Lane) alley sells old-fashioned Japanese sweets from small family shops. Significantly fewer international visitors than Kamakura; similar historical atmosphere.

Chichibu

Chichibu, 90 minutes from Ikebukuro on the Seibu Chichibu Line. A mountain town with a famous Shinto shrine (Chichibu Shrine), a series of 34 Buddhist pilgrimage temples that serious walkers circuit over multiple days, and the only natural karst formation in the Kanto region at Ootaki. In December, the Chichibu Night Festival (December 2窶・) is one of Japan's largest float festivals and draws 200,000 people 窶・still far more manageable than any equivalent Tokyo-area event.

Izu Peninsula

Izu Peninsula, 90 minutes from Tokyo by Shinkansen to Atami or Mishima. A volcanic peninsula with hot springs, dramatic coastline, and fewer tourists per kilometer than anywhere in the Hakone area. Shuzenji in the central valley is a small onsen town with a classic feel 窶・a bamboo grove along the river, wooden onsen hotels, a 9th-century Buddhist temple. Enough for a full day without a crowd problem.

Mashiko

Mashiko, two hours from Ueno by train (JR Utsunomiya plus bus). A ceramics town that became famous when the potter Shoji Hamada established a studio there in the 1920s and invited other ceramicists. There are now around 300 kilns in and around the town. The spring and autumn pottery fairs are among the best craft markets in Japan. The Hamada Shoji Memorial Mashiko Sankokan museum is on the original estate and worth two hours. The journey requires planning but the experience is unique.
