The Rules of Gift Giving in Japan: What to Give, How to Give It, When Not To

Gift giving in Japan is governed by a set of conventions that are invisible until you get them wrong. Here is what they are.

In this article

  1. The presentation matters as much as the gift
  2. Numbers and colors to avoid
  3. Gifts are not opened in front of the giver
  4. Giving with two hands
  5. What makes a good gift
  6. When not to give a gift

The presentation matters as much as the gift

In Japan, the wrapping of a gift is not separate from the gift 窶・it is part of it. A well-chosen item presented unwrapped or in a plastic bag conveys carelessness. A modest item in beautiful wrapping conveys consideration. Department stores in Japan wrap purchases without being asked, and the wrapping is typically done with a precision that takes skill. If you are bringing a gift from abroad, find a way to present it wrapped. The intent is not to deceive about value; it is to communicate that you thought about the presentation.

Numbers and colors to avoid

Avoid gifts in sets of four (shi sounds like the word for death) or nine (ku sounds like the word for suffering). Sets of three, five, or seven are considered auspicious. White wrapping paper, used in funeral contexts, and black and white ribbon combinations are better avoided for celebratory gifts. Red is generally acceptable. Gold is particularly positive.

Gifts are not opened in front of the giver

The Japanese convention is to receive a gift graciously, set it aside, and open it privately later. Opening a gift immediately in front of the person who gave it 窶・examining it, exclaiming over it, asking about the price 窶・is not standard. If a Japanese host opens your gift immediately, it is a gesture of warmth toward you specifically. If they set it aside, do not interpret this as indifference. The gift has been received and will be appreciated; the appreciation is simply expressed differently.

Giving with two hands

Gifts, business cards, money envelopes, and any item given as a gesture of significance should be presented with both hands, accompanied by a slight bow. Receiving should be the same. This is not always possible with large items, but the gesture of offering both hands signals respect and intention. Handing something with one hand while doing something else with the other is fine in a casual context and reads as slightly inattentive in a formal one.

What makes a good gift

The best gifts from abroad: regional foods that can't be bought in Japan, quality items associated with your home country, or something that reflects genuine thought about the recipient. Luxury goods as gifts place the recipient in an uncomfortable position (they feel obligated to reciprocate at an equivalent level). Practical consumables 窶・food, drink, quality cosmetics 窶・avoid this dynamic. The rule of thumb used by Japanese people for omiyage applies equally here: regional, edible, individually portioned, appropriately priced.

When not to give a gift

Do not tip in lieu of a gift. Do not give a gift to someone in a customer service role unless you have an established personal relationship with them. Do not give a gift that clearly exceeds the social context 窶・a very expensive present to a casual acquaintance places an obligation on them that is uncomfortable. The gift economy in Japan operates on calibration: the value of the gift reflects the relationship, and mismatching either direction creates awkwardness.
