The Tokyo Subway: How to Navigate It Without Getting Lost

Tokyo窶冱 train network is the most complex urban rail system in the world. It is also, once you understand the basics, extremely easy to use.

Two networks, one system

Tokyo窶冱 rail network is operated by multiple companies, of which two matter most to visitors: Tokyo Metro (13 lines, run by Tokyo Metro Co.) and Toei Subway (4 lines, run by the Tokyo Metropolitan Government). They connect at major stations, but they are separate systems with separate fares 窶・transferring between them costs extra unless you have an IC card, which handles the fare calculation automatically. On an IC card, the transfer cost is minimal and seamless. On a paper ticket, you窶冤l need to buy a new one each time. Get the IC card.

The Yamanote Line

The Yamanote Line is not a subway 窶・it窶冱 a JR surface rail loop that runs around central Tokyo, connecting Shinjuku, Shibuya, Harajuku, Ebisu, Osaki, Shinagawa, Tokyo, Akihabara, Ueno, Nippori, Ikebukuro, and back. It takes about an hour to complete the full loop. Think of it as your outer ring: when you want to move between major neighborhoods, this is usually how you do it. Frequency during peak hours is every two to three minutes.

IC card vs paper ticket

Buy a Suica IC card at any JR ticket machine at the airport or a major station. Load it with ﾂ･2,000窶・,000 to start. Tap in, tap out, and the fare is deducted automatically. You never need to calculate fares or buy destination-specific tickets. A paper ticket requires you to select the exact destination and correct fare before you board, which requires reading a map that is entirely in Japanese at some smaller stations. There is no scenario where a paper ticket is preferable to an IC card.

Reading the maps

Tokyo Metro maps are color-coded by line and numbered by station. Each line has a letter prefix and each station a number (e.g., G07 for Ginza on the Ginza line). This number system is displayed on platform signs and inside carriages, which means you can navigate without reading kanji at all. The printed maps at train stations are free and multi-language. Inside the carriages, digital displays above the doors show the next station in Japanese, English, Chinese, and Korean.

Google Maps

Google Maps with transit mode is accurate and reliable for Tokyo public transport. Enter your destination, select the transit option, and it will show you the correct line, platform, number of stops, and transfer instructions. The journey planner also shows fare estimates. Use it as your primary navigation tool. Japan-specific apps like HyperDia and Navitime are more detailed for complex Shinkansen bookings but offer nothing additional for basic subway navigation.

The last train

Tokyo窶冱 trains stop running around midnight (times vary by line and station; check Google Maps for the exact last train). This is not like New York or London where the service continues through the night with reduced frequency. The trains stop, full stop. Taxis are available but expensive after midnight and in short supply on Friday and Saturday nights. If you窶决e out late, plan your route in advance and know your last train time. Getting stranded at midnight in Shibuya is solvable but inconvenient and expensive.
