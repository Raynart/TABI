Taking a Taxi in Japan: What to Know Before You Get In

Japanese taxis are expensive, exceptionally clean, and almost entirely driven by people who know exactly where they are going. The main things to know are not about navigation.

  2. Show the destination in writing
  3. Fares and metering
  4. When to use a taxi
  5. Hailing and apps

The door opens automatically

Japanese taxi doors open and close automatically, controlled by the driver. Do not touch the door. Attempting to open or close it manually will work but is unnecessary and slightly startles the driver. This catches almost every first-time visitor. Stand near the door, wait for it to open, get in. When you arrive, the driver will open it for you. Get out, step away, the door closes.

Show the destination in writing

Most taxi drivers in Japan speak limited English. The reliable approach: have your destination written in Japanese characters, either on your phone (from Google Maps, copy the Japanese name) or on a piece of paper from your hotel. Pronunciation of Western place names in Japanese is often different enough from English pronunciation to cause confusion. A screen showing the map with the destination pinned is universally understood. Your hotel's front desk should always be able to provide you with a card showing the hotel address in Japanese 窶・ask for one at check-in.

Credit cards are accepted in most Tokyo taxis now; IC cards (Suica) are accepted in many. Check the payment stickers on the rear side window before you get in if you're unsure.

When to use a taxi

Taxis are worth the cost in specific situations: after the last train, with heavy luggage between hotel and station, when traveling with elderly or young children, in cities with infrequent bus service, or when the destination is not near a train line. In Tokyo during normal hours, taxis are significantly more expensive than the train and not faster 窶・traffic density means trains win on time and price for most inner-city routes. Outside Tokyo, in smaller cities, taxis become more valuable as transit alternatives are thinner.

Hailing and apps

Taxis in Japan are hailed from the street 窶・stand at the curb, extend your arm, and a cab with a lit red indicator (available for hire) will stop. Do not try to hail a cab at a zebra crossing or intersection. Taxi apps: GO (Japan-specific, major cities, English available), Uber (operates in some Japanese cities but uses licensed taxis rather than private drivers), and DiDi. GO is the most widely used and works in Tokyo, Osaka, Kyoto, and most other large cities. Having it installed before you need it at midnight is a better plan than finding it at midnight.
