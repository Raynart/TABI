Your First Ryokan: What to Expect, What to Do, and What Not to Wear

A traditional Japanese inn is not a hotel. The sooner you accept that, the better the stay.

Check-in, checkout, and why timing matters

The check-in time at a ryokan is usually 3pm and checkout is 10am. These aren't suggestions. Your room is being transformed twice a day — futon laid out at turndown, rolled away before breakfast — and that choreography is coordinated with dinner and bath times. Arriving at 6pm and expecting your room to be ready the way a hotel room is always ready will result in confusion on both sides.

Shoes, slippers, and one rule about the toilet

When you arrive, you remove your shoes at the entrance and switch into slippers. The slippers go everywhere except tatami rooms — in tatami rooms you go barefoot or in socks. There will be a separate pair of toilet slippers in the bathroom. Put them on to enter, take them off to leave. Forgetting and wandering the hallway in toilet slippers is the ryokan equivalent of walking through a dinner party in a life jacket.

Your room

Your room will have: a low table, zabuton cushions, a tokonoma (a small alcove, usually with a seasonal arrangement), a yukata folded somewhere, and a kettle with tea. The yukata is your uniform. Wear it to the onsen, to dinner if the place is traditional, back to your room. It's a cotton robe, not a costume. Left side over right — right over left is how the dead are dressed.

The onsen

The onsen, if there is one, operates on a schedule — typically gender-segregated, sometimes with rotating access to different baths. The rules: wash thoroughly at the shower station before entering the communal water. Don't bring a towel into the bath. The small hand towel goes on your head or beside the tub. Don't get in if you've been drinking heavily — people faint, and it's embarrassing for everyone.

Kaiseki dinner

Dinner is kaiseki — a multi-course meal built around seasonal ingredients. It arrives in a sequence you don't choose and can't rush. Don't ask for the menu in advance. Don't request variations unless you have a genuine allergy. The meal takes 90 minutes to two hours, the plates will be small, and you will feel, briefly, like you're not getting enough food. You are getting enough food. The point is rhythm.

Breakfast

Breakfast follows the same logic: rice, miso soup, pickled vegetables, grilled fish, tofu, a small egg dish. It arrives at a fixed time, usually between 7:30 and 9am. It is also the best breakfast in the world, and I say this as someone who is not generally enthusiastic about breakfast.

What it costs

Prices run from around ¥8,000 per person at a decent mid-range option with onsen and breakfast to ¥80,000+ per person at the higher end with full kaiseki. The sweet spot is in the ¥15,000–¥25,000 range — particularly in onsen towns like Kinosaki Onsen in Hyogo or Gero in Gifu, which don't have the premium of Hakone or Kyoto but are genuinely excellent.

On language

A final note on language. The staff will likely not speak much English, and that's genuinely fine. The experience requires very little language to work. 'Arigatou gozaimasu' (thank you very much) and 'oishii' (delicious) will handle 80% of situations. The rest comes from gestures, patience, and the fact that the people running a ryokan have been doing this for decades and know how to communicate without words.
