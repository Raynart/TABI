Don Quijote: What to Buy, What to Skip, and How to Survive the Store

Don Quijote is five floors of everything at prices that make no sense. The key is knowing what you came for before you walk in.

In this article

  1. What Don Quijote is
  2. Skincare and cosmetics
  3. Japanese snacks
  4. Electronics and gadgets
  5. What to skip
  6. Navigation

What Don Quijote is

Don Quijote — called Donki by everyone who shops there — is a discount variety store that operates on the principle that if you put enough products in a small enough space at a low enough price, people will buy things they didn't know they needed. This works. The stores are open until 5am. The in-store music is a jingle that plays on loop. The lighting is aggressive. It is not a relaxing shopping experience. It is an effective one.

Skincare and cosmetics

What to buy. Skincare and cosmetics: Donki carries Japanese drugstore brands (Hada Labo, Kose, Shiseido) and some Korean imports at prices below anywhere else you'll find them in Japan. The Hada Labo Gokujyun moisturizing lotion (170ml, around ¥500) is the specific item that beauty content creators have been writing about for years, and the Donki price is genuinely the best in Tokyo. Load up.

Japanese snacks

Japanese snacks: the basement or ground floor of most Donki has a food section with Kit Kat flavors, Pocky, regional chips, and gift-packaged snack sets. If you're buying snacks to take home, this is more convenient than tracking down a specialty snack shop and often cheaper. Green tea Kit Kat in the standard retail package at around ¥300 a box is a reasonable gift that travels well.

Electronics and gadgets

Electronics and gadgets: the electronics section has genuine deals on portable chargers, travel adapters, and USB cables. Don't buy anything complex — laptops and cameras here require Japanese region settings and Japanese warranties. The accessories are fine.

What to skip

What to skip: branded luxury goods in the glass cases near the register. These are legitimate products but the selection is limited and the prices aren't as competitive as the rest of the store. Perfumes are hit or miss. For Japanese whisky, sake, or shochu worth buying, go to a dedicated liquor store.

Navigation

Navigation: the store layout is deliberately non-intuitive. Signage exists but products are stacked floor-to-ceiling in a way that turns navigation into something between a maze and an archaeological dig. Accept this. Give yourself at least 90 minutes for your first visit. Don't try to optimize; let the store find you things. The Shinjuku and Shibuya branches are the largest and most complete. The Akihabara branch is useful if you want the electronics section without the full Donki experience.
