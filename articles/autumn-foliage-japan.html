Autumn Leaves in Japan: Where to Go and When the Timing Actually Works

Koyo — autumn foliage — is not a fixed event. It starts in Hokkaido in September and ends in Kyushu in December. The timing requires a decision.

In this article

  2. Kyoto in November
  3. Nikko: the underrated option
  4. The Japanese Alps
  5. Rain and overcast days
  6. The practical window

When koyo happens where

Japan's autumn color season (koyo) moves southward and downhill from September through late November. Hokkaido peaks first — late September to mid-October — while central Honshu (Nikko, the Japanese Alps, Kyoto) peaks in early to mid-November. Kyushu and the warmer lowlands finish last, sometimes into December. 'When is the best time for autumn leaves?' depends entirely on where you're going, and the answer shifts by two to four weeks depending on whether you're in the mountains or at sea level.

Kyoto in November

Kyoto's koyo season typically peaks in the second and third weeks of November. The problem: so does Kyoto's tourist density. Tenryu-ji in Arashiyama, Eikan-do in Nanzen-ji, the garden at Tofuku-ji — all of these are spectacular and all of them will be packed. The answer is not to avoid them (the crowds exist because the places are worth it) but to arrange your visit around timing. These gardens look different at 7:30am than at 11am. The light is better and the ratio of leaves to people is significantly higher.

Nikko: the underrated option

The underrated alternative: Nikko, two hours by express from Ueno in Tokyo. The cryptomeria avenue leading to Tosho-gu Shrine, surrounded by beech and maple turning gold and orange, is one of the better koyo experiences in Japan and receives a fraction of Kyoto's November visitors. The Irohazaka switchback road to Lake Chuzenji is the specific spot — worth the drive if you have transport, or a bus if you don't.

The Japanese Alps

The Japanese Alps for serious foliage: Kamikochi (closed after mid-November), Shirakawa-go, and the Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route offer high-altitude autumn that peaks in October and is genuinely spectacular. Shirakawa-go's thatched-roof farmhouses surrounded by autumn maples is the kind of composition that doesn't require a good photographer — the scene does the work.

Rain and overcast days

What nobody says: rain makes autumn foliage better. The colors are more saturated. The light through wet maple leaves is different from the light through dry ones. An overcast day in Kyoto in November is not a failed day; it's a different photograph and a quieter street. Adjust expectations and pack accordingly.

The practical window

The practical window: if you have flexibility in your travel dates, aim for November 10–20 for central Honshu. Before that, the color is incomplete. After that, the leaves have begun to fall and you're watching the end of something. The narrow peak is what makes koyo worth planning around.
