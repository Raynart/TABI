Japanese Whisky: What's Worth Buying and What's Just Marketing

The golden age is over but the whisky is still exceptional. You just need to know what you're actually looking at.

Somewhere around 2014, the world decided Japanese whisky was the best in the world. Suntory's Yamazaki 12 Year won a prominent international award. Whisky writers made pilgrimages to Nikka's distillery in Hokkaido. By 2016 you couldn't find a bottle of Yamazaki Single Malt for under ¥15,000 in a Tokyo duty-free. By 2020 you couldn't find it at all.

The scarcity problem is real but overstated. What happened: global demand exploded, Japanese distilleries — which had been producing at a domestic-consumption pace — couldn't scale fast enough. The aged expressions ran dry. Secondary market prices went insane. But the distilleries kept working, and the whisky that was aging in 2016 and 2018 is now maturing. Availability is slowly improving.

What's genuinely worth buying in 2026. Nikka From The Barrel: a blended whisky that consistently outperforms its price. Around ¥3,000 at a convenience store if you're lucky enough to find it. Suntory Toki: an affordable blend designed for highballs — order it at a bar before you commit to a bottle, which will take about one sip. Any single malt expression from Yamazaki or Yoichi that you can find at close to retail price rather than secondary market markup.

The highball is the format. Japanese whisky culture is built around the whisky highball — spirit over ice with soda, served in a tall frosted glass. It sounds humble. It isn't. Order one at any decent whisky bar before you start shopping; it will calibrate your sense of what you're buying.

Where to buy: department store basement floors (depachika) and specialist liquor stores have the best selection and prices. Airport duty-free is convenient but the offering has narrowed significantly since the shortage began. Avoid hotel gift shops. The markup is severe and the selection is whatever moves fastest with tourists.

On labeling: 'Japanese whisky' now has legal standards, introduced in 2021. The spirit must be made in Japan from Japanese ingredients, malted and aged in Japan, and bottled in Japan. Before this regulation, some brands were blending imported Scottish or Canadian spirit and calling it Japanese. The rules now require transparency. If a bottle says 'blended' without a specific distillery name, read the fine print.

What you're most likely to actually find and afford: Suntory Kakubin (yellow label, a classic blend, excellent for highballs, around ¥1,200 at most liquor shops), Nikka Coffey Malt or Coffey Grain (made in a Coffey still, unusual and worth the ¥7,000), and possibly Chichibu expressions from Ichiro's Malt if you're prepared to spend ¥15,000 or more on something genuinely scarce.

One bottle to look for specifically: Mars Komagatake, from the highest-altitude distillery in Japan, located in Nagano. Production is limited and domestic allocation is small, but it shows up in specialist shops and occasionally at airport duty-free. It's not as famous as Yamazaki, but the gap in quality doesn't justify the gap in price and availability. Find it while that's still true.
