Eating at a Sushi Counter: What to Expect and How to Not Ruin It

The sushi counter is not intimidating once you understand that there are three rules, they're easy, and the chef wants you to enjoy it.

Counter sushi vs conveyor belt

Counter sushi (omakase or a la carte) is different from kaiten (conveyor belt) in format, price, and the nature of the relationship between you and the chef. At the counter, there are usually 8窶・2 seats and a clear glass case of today's fish. You either order a set omakase (chef's choice, typically ﾂ･5,000窶・0,000 per person) or point at items in the case and they're made in front of you. The fish was at the market this morning. The rice is slightly warm. The whole operation is built around immediacy.

Three rules

Three rules. One: eat the sushi as soon as it's placed in front of you. The rice is seasoned and tempered for eating now, not in three minutes. Sushi that sits at room temperature changes. Two: it is acceptable to eat nigiri with your fingers. This is actually the traditional method. Using chopsticks risks the topping sliding off and is optional. Three: dip fish-side down into soy sauce, not rice-side down (soy sauce in the rice makes it fall apart and oversalts the bite).

What to order

What to order if you can. Tuna in its hierarchy: chutoro (medium fatty tuna) over maguro (lean tuna) over otoro (full fatty tuna) 窶・not because otoro isn't extraordinary, but because it's overwhelming in large quantities. The best first piece at a sushi counter is usually whatever the chef puts in front of you first, which is often a lighter white fish like hirame (flounder) or suzuki (sea bass) designed to calibrate your palate.

Ginger and wasabi

Ginger (gari) is a palate cleanser, eaten between pieces, not on top of them. Wasabi is incorporated into the sushi by the chef at a traditional counter 窶・additional wasabi on the side is available but signals that you found the amount insufficient. This is fine. Ask.

Budget guidance

Budget guidance: the most common misconception about counter sushi in Japan is that it's prohibitively expensive. Tsukiji Outer Market, Ueno, and the side streets of Shinjuku have the highest density of good-value options.
