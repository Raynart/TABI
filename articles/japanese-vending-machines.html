Japan's Vending Machines Are a Serious Subject

There are 4 million vending machines in Japan. One for every 31 people. This is not an accident.

In this article

  2. What is for sale
  3. Specialty machines
  4. Pricing
  5. IC card payment
  6. The cultural point

The density

Japan has more vending machines per capita than any country except possibly Singapore. They appear outside train stations, on mountain trails, inside office buildings, at the end of rural roads where nothing else exists for kilometers. The density is a logistics achievement: the machines are serviced on fixed schedules, calibrated to local demand, and stocked with whatever the neighborhood apparently wants at the hour it apparently wants it.

What is for sale

What's for sale: the most common machines stock canned and bottled drinks — hot canned coffee, cold green tea, sports drinks, canned beer, occasionally alcohol. The hot/cold switching happens seasonally and sometimes twice daily — a button will show HOT or COLD depending on which side of the internal temperature shelf the product is resting on. Buying a hot Boss Coffee from a vending machine in February is one of those simple pleasures that people consistently describe as an unexpectedly significant memory from Japan.

Specialty machines

The specialty machines: some train stations have instant ramen machines with a surprisingly wide variety. Umbrella machines appear outside train stations when rain is forecast and charge ¥500–800 for a basic transparent umbrella. There are machines selling fresh bananas, hot pizza by the slice, live stag beetles in summer (this is real), and surgical masks in quantities ranging from one to fifty.

Pricing

Pricing: drinks at vending machines are ¥130–200 for most items, approximately the same as a convenience store. The premium is zero or marginal. Alcohol (beer, chuhai) is available late-night where the machine is located outside a convenience store or near a station — age verification is on the honor system at the machine level, though some have a button for adult confirmation.

IC card payment

IC card payment: most modern vending machines accept Suica or ICOCA. Tap the card reader, the drink drops, and the amount is deducted. It's faster than coins, which in Japan are otherwise still the default payment method for vending. The transition to contactless is quicker here than at cash registers, perhaps because the machine doesn't need to be retrained.

The cultural point

The cultural point: vending machines in Japan are clean, they work (machine failure is rare enough to be noteworthy), and they stock things people actually want. This seems basic until you've been somewhere where vending machines are a coin trap of expired merchandise and broken change returns. Japan's machines are infrastructure, and like most Japanese infrastructure, they are taken seriously.
