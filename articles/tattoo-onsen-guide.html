Tattoos and Onsen in Japan: What the Policy Actually Is

Most onsen ban tattoos. Fewer than you'd think actually enforce it. Here is how to navigate it honestly.

In this article

  1. Why the ban exists
  2. The official policy vs practice
  3. Options if you have tattoos
  4. The honest approach
  5. Changing attitudes

Why the ban exists

The tattoo ban at Japanese onsen traces to the historical association between full-body tattoos (irezumi) and organized crime. The ban was introduced not as a cultural statement about tattoos generally but as a practical measure to keep criminal organizations out of communal bathing facilities. The policy has remained for decades largely through institutional inertia, though attitudes have shifted significantly since the Tokyo Olympics announcement in 2013 brought the issue into public debate.

The official policy vs practice

Official policy at most large resort onsen and hotel spa facilities: no tattoos, regardless of size. Enforcement varies significantly. At a small neighborhood sento (public bath) run by a family for 40 years, nobody is checking. At a large facility near a major tourist area, staff may ask at the entrance. The primary concern has always been the communal nature of the bathing 窶・a heavily tattooed person in a visible position in a shared bath creates discomfort for other guests who have the historical association in mind.

Options if you have tattoos

Private baths (kashikiri buro or kashi-kiriburo): many ryokan and onsen hotels offer private family baths bookable by the hour 窶・a full bath for your group only, with no other guests and therefore no policy. This is often the most relaxing option regardless. Tattoo-friendly facilities: they exist and are increasing in number. Websites like Tattoo-Friendly Japan (search for this specifically) maintain updated lists of facilities with explicit tattoo-welcome policies. Covering: some facilities allow tattoos covered by a waterproof bandage or tattoo cover sheet (sold at some facilities), but this is only workable for small tattoos and not universal.

The honest approach

The most straightforward path: contact any onsen facility you intend to visit in advance and ask. Most facilities have dealt with this question before and can give you a direct answer. Arriving at a facility and discovering the policy on entry, then having a confrontation at the desk, is unpleasant for everyone. The honest approach 窶・ask first, respect the answer 窶・is the correct one regardless of your personal opinion about the policy.

Changing attitudes

Japan's tattoo culture is genuinely changing. Younger Japanese people increasingly have tattoos. The Japan Tourism Agency has encouraged onsen facilities to reconsider blanket bans in favor of more nuanced approaches, particularly given international visitor numbers. Some facilities now operate on a no-visible-tattoo basis rather than a full ban. The situation in 2026 is meaningfully different from 2015, and will continue to evolve. The number of tattoo-friendly facilities available is increasing every year.
