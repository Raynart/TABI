Where to Stay in Tokyo: Honest Assessments of Each Neighborhood

Every first-time visitor books Shinjuku because someone told them to. Some of those people are right.

In this article

  1. Why neighborhood choice matters
  2. Shinjuku
  3. Asakusa
  4. Shibuya
  5. Ginza
  6. Ueno
  7. The general rule

Why neighborhood choice matters

Tokyo has 23 special wards and dozens of distinct neighborhoods, each with a different character and a different argument for why you should sleep there. The decision matters more than most hotel comparison sites suggest. An hour on the Yamanote Line from Ueno to Shibuya is not the same as a ten-minute walk from wherever you're standing. Where you sleep shapes what you do in the morning.

Shinjuku

Shinjuku: the right choice for first-timers who want maximum convenience. Shinjuku Station connects to almost everything. The west side has the skyscraper district and the Keio bus terminal for day trips. The east side has Kabukicho, Golden Gai, and Omoide Yokocho 窶・three different things to do at night. Hotels range from budget capsule options in the ﾂ･3,000窶・,000/night range to Park Hyatt at the top. The area is not charming, but it works.

Asakusa

Asakusa: the tourist district, with one of the city's best sightseeing densities within walking distance (Senso-ji, Nakamise, the Sumida River). The downside is that it's quieter outside tourist hours, transport options are limited compared to Shinjuku or Shibuya, and the neighborhood doesn't have the density of restaurants and bars that other areas do. Good for first-timers wanting concentrated sightseeing and a vaguely traditional atmosphere. Less good for eating out late or getting to Shibuya quickly.

Shibuya

Shibuya: better for people on a second or third visit. The neighborhood is in constant construction (it's been under renovation since 2012 and will remain so until approximately 2027). The crossing is iconic and worth seeing once; it's not worth basing a week around. The advantage is easy access to the private rail lines heading west 窶・Shimokitazawa on the Keio Inokashira Line, Nakameguro and Daikanyama within walking distance.

Ginza

Ginza: expensive, convenient, quiet at night. If your priorities are ease of getting around and you're not interested in nightlife, the business hotels here are excellent value relative to the address. Ginza Line goes directly to Shibuya. Walking distance to Tsukiji. Largely tourists and executives, which can feel sterile.

Ueno

Ueno: the most practical choice for travelers with a lot of luggage arriving into Ueno Station, which has direct Shinkansen connections from the north. The area around Ueno Park is dense with museums and cheap restaurants. It has none of Shinjuku's energy, but it has a functioning neighborhood character that Shinjuku lacks 窶・Yanaka and Nezu are walkable from here and are among the most interesting old-city districts in Tokyo.

The general rule

The general rule: within the Yamanote Line loop, you're never more than 20 minutes from anything important. The choice of neighborhood is about what you walk outside to in the morning, not about access. Pick based on the atmosphere you want for breakfast and evening, and trust the train system to handle the rest.
