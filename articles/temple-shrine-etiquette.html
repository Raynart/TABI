How to Visit a Japanese Temple or Shrine Without Being Accidentally Rude

Temples and shrines have different rules, different purposes, and different gods. Getting them confused is the starting mistake. Everything else follows from there.

Temple vs shrine 遯ｶ繝ｻthe difference matters

Temples (o-tera) are Buddhist. Shrines (jinja) are Shinto. The buildings look different, the rituals are different, and the correct behavior is slightly different at each. The fastest way to tell them apart: if there is a torii gate 遯ｶ繝ｻthe distinctive arch, usually red or stone 遯ｶ繝ｻyou are at a shrine. If there is a large incense burner and a main hall with Buddha imagery, you are at a temple. Many famous sites have both on the same grounds, particularly in Kyoto, because Buddhism and Shinto coexisted for centuries.

The torii gate

Walking through a torii gate marks the transition from the ordinary world into sacred space. Walk through it, not around it. The center of the path through a shrine is considered the route of the deity; some people walk slightly to one side as a gesture of deference. Pour the remaining water from the ladle over both hands simultaneously, tilting the ladle so the water runs down the handle rather than being poured from the bowl. Photography

Photography of the exterior grounds is almost always permitted. Photography inside the main hall is often not, and there will usually be a sign. If there is no sign and you are unsure, look at what other visitors are doing. Taking a photograph while someone else is praying is poor form regardless of whether signs permit it.
