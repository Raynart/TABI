Japan's Four Major Ramen Styles: What They Are and Where to Actually Eat Them

Ramen in Japan is not one food. Depending on where you are, the word covers a range of things that share almost nothing except noodles.

  2. Hakata tonkotsu

  4. Sapporo miso
  5. How to order

Four styles, not one

The four canonical regional ramen styles in Japan are Sapporo (miso), Hakata (tonkotsu), Tokyo (shoyu), and Kitakata (shoyu with flat noodles). This is a simplification. Within each region there are dozens of substyles, proprietary broths, and variations by shop. But the four regional types give you a framework that is more useful than 'ramen is ramen.'

Hakata tonkotsu

Hakata ramen from Fukuoka is the style most likely to become your obsession. The broth is made from pork bones (tonkotsu) simmered for 12 to 18 hours until it turns milky white and rich enough to coat a spoon. The noodles are thin and straight, cooked harder than you'd expect. The correct serving size is small — a half-portion (kogata) is standard, and you're expected to order kaedama (a noodle refill) into the remaining broth. Shops around Hakata Station and the yatai stalls along the Naka River are where to eat it. Budget ¥700–1,000.

Tokyo shoyu

Tokyo shoyu ramen is the gentler style. Chicken or pork-based broth seasoned with soy sauce, usually clearer than Hakata broth, with medium wavy noodles. The toppings are what distinguish one Tokyo shop from another: chashu pork, a soft-boiled egg marinated in soy and mirin, bamboo shoots, nori. The shop Fuunji in Shinjuku makes a tsukemen (dipping ramen) version that requires arriving at 11am or accepting a 45-minute wait.

Sapporo miso

Sapporo miso ramen is the winter version — a heavier broth with miso paste stirred in, often with butter, sweet corn, and bean sprouts on top. It makes sense in Hokkaido in November in a way it doesn't in Tokyo in August. The Susukino neighborhood in Sapporo has the highest concentration of shops worth trying. Expect to spend ¥900–1,200.

How to order

How to order at a ramen shop: most have ticket vending machines at the entrance. Put in your money, select your bowl, take the ticket, and hand it to staff when you sit. If you want a soft-boiled egg (onsen tamago or ajitama), it's usually a separate button. Noodle firmness (kata for firm, yawarakai for soft) and richness (koi for heavy, asa for light) can be specified at some shops. Do not over-specify at your first visit. Order the standard and learn what you're tasting before you start adjusting.
