Hokkaido in July: Japan's Best-Kept Summer Secret

Tokyo in July is 35 degrees and 90% humidity. Hokkaido in July is 22 degrees and clear. These two facts explain a lot.

The climate case

Hokkaido is Japan's northernmost island and its least densely populated. The summer climate is a direct consequence of this geography: while central Honshu is enduring a summer that most temperate-climate visitors find difficult, Hokkaido is experiencing something close to a Northern European July. Average temperatures in Sapporo in July run 22窶・5ﾂｰC, with lower humidity. It's possible to walk outside without immediately sweating through your clothes. For visitors arriving from humid climates, this discovery is approximately life-changing.

Lavender fields in Furano

The lavender fields of Furano are the most photographed image of Hokkaido summer: long rows of purple lavender against a backdrop of hills and clouds, peaking in early to mid-July. Farm Tomita in Furano is the most famous, and is legitimately beautiful despite being a tourist operation. Come early morning. The crowds arrive by 10am. The lavender at 7am with mist still on the hills is the real version of the photograph you've seen.

Biei and the Panorama Road

Biei: a small town 20 kilometers north of Furano with rolling farmland 窶・patchwork fields of potato, wheat, sunflower, and canola 窶・that attracts photographers specifically for the quality of the light in summer. Renting a bicycle and spending the morning on the Panorama Road is the correct approach. No specific sight to reach; the road and the fields are the destination.

Sapporo

Sapporo: the capital and the city that functions as the base. The Odori Park beer garden runs all summer (July窶鄭ugust) 窶・rows of tables under the elm trees with brewery stalls from Sapporo, Asahi, Kirin, and Suntory running simultaneously. This is worth a single evening on its own terms. Soup curry, a Sapporo invention, is served in most restaurants here and is one of those regional dishes that makes no sense until you've eaten it.

Daisetsuzan National Park

The Daisetsuzan National Park: the largest national park in Japan, an alpine region accessible from multiple trailheads. The ropeway at Asahidake gives access to volcanic terrain above 1,500 meters where the hiking is excellent and the views extend across the island. A serious day-hike into the high terrain will not intersect with many other people and looks nothing like the rest of Japan.

Once you're there, a rental car is the right choice for Furano and Biei; trains exist but the timing is inconvenient for covering multiple areas in a day.
