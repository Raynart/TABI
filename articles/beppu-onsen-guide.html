Beppu: The Onsen City That's Stranger and Better Than You Expect

Beppu produces more geothermal steam per day than anywhere in Japan and possibly anywhere outside Iceland. The city treats this as completely normal.

Beppu is on the northeast coast of Kyushu, about an hour and a half from Fukuoka by limited express train. The city has eight distinct onsen zones (onsen hatto), each with different mineral compositions and water colors — clear, milky white, reddish brown, and the vivid cobalt blue of the Kannawa area. The steam rising off the streets in certain neighborhoods on cool mornings makes it look like the city is slowly cooking from below, which is more or less accurate.

The Jigoku Meguri — the Hell Tour — is the tourist attraction: seven pools of geothermally active water that can't be bathed in because they're too hot (98°C in some cases) or too acidic. The pools are genuinely remarkable. Umi Jigoku (Sea Hell) is deep blue and appears to be 200 meters wide. Chinoike Jigoku (Blood Pond Hell) is exactly as red as it sounds. Oniyama Jigoku contains a crocodile enclosure because the groundwater is warm enough to maintain tropical reptiles year-round. A circuit ticket covers all seven for ¥2,200. It's worth it.

The actual bathing options are the reason to stay overnight. Kannawa and the older neighborhood onsens offer public baths (kotoyu) for as little as ¥100–200 — coin-operated, simple tiled rooms, water that does things to your skin that hotel onsen doesn't quite replicate. Hyotan Onsen near Kannawa has eight distinct pools and won a prestigious onsen award; ¥1,300 for full access, open until 1am.

Sand bathing: Takegawara Onsen in central Beppu offers sunayu — you lie in a shallow pit and staff bury you in geothermally heated sand. The sand temperature is around 50°C, which sounds alarming and is instead comfortable in a way that's difficult to explain until you've experienced it. Fifteen minutes in the sand is roughly equivalent to an hour in a conventional bath in terms of post-session relaxation. ¥1,500, no appointment needed.

Getting there and where to stay: the Sonic or Sonata limited express from Hakata (Fukuoka) takes 1h45m–2h and costs around ¥4,500 (or covered by JR Pass). The city is small enough that the main onsen areas are walkable from the station or a ¥700 taxi ride. Ryokan here are genuinely affordable compared to Hakone — ¥10,000–15,000 per person including dinner and breakfast is achievable. Beppu makes a strong case for being the most accessible serious onsen town in Japan.
