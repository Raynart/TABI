How to Use Chopsticks in Japan 窶・and What Never to Do

Using chopsticks correctly in Japan is less about technique and more about knowing the four things that are genuinely offensive. The technique is optional. The four things are not.

In this article

  1. The four things you must not do
  2. Passing food chopstick to chopstick
  3. Technique is not the point
  4. Picking up communal dishes
  5. Disposable chopsticks

The four things you must not do

Tatehashi: standing chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice. This is how rice is offered to the dead at funerals. It is considered deeply inauspicious at a dining table. If you need to put your chopsticks down, use the rest (hashioki), or rest them across the bowl edge. Watashi-hashi: resting chopsticks across the top of a bowl as a signal you are done. This is acceptable in practice, but the correct signal is to set them on the hashioki. Sashi-hashi: spearing food with a chopstick as if it were a fork. For things that are difficult to pick up, ask for a fork 窶・it's available at most restaurants. Mayoi-hashi: hovering chopsticks over dishes while deciding what to take. Pick up from the dish you are pointing at.

Passing food chopstick to chopstick

Passing food directly from one person's chopsticks to another's is associated with the funeral ritual of passing cremated bones. If you want to share food, place it on the other person's plate. This rule is known to most Japanese people and explained to children; seeing foreigners do it is understood as unfamiliarity rather than disrespect, but it is worth knowing.

Technique is not the point

Many restaurants that serve foreign visitors have forks available on request. If you cannot use chopsticks comfortably, ask for a fork. Nobody will be offended. Struggling badly with chopsticks for an entire meal while declining a fork is not a gesture of cultural respect 窶・it is inconvenient for you and creates mild concern for the people eating with you. Use the fork. Practice the chopsticks when you have time and aren't hungry.

Picking up communal dishes

At a shared meal 窶・izakaya, shabu-shabu, hot pot 窶・there may be serving chopsticks (toribashi) for transferring food from the central dish to your plate. If there aren't, the convention is to use the reverse end of your own chopsticks (the end you haven't had in your mouth) for serving. This is understood and widely practiced in informal settings.

Disposable chopsticks

Waribashi are the disposable wooden chopsticks served at most casual restaurants and convenience stores. You split them apart from their joined end. Rubbing them together to remove splinters is considered poor form at a proper restaurant 窶・it implies the chopsticks are of low quality, which implies the restaurant is of low quality. At a convenience store eating lunch on a bench, this rule is not in operation. Context applies.
