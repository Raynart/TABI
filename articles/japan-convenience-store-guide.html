The Convenience Store Is Japan's Greatest Culinary Achievement

Seven-Eleven in Japan is not Seven-Eleven anywhere else. This distinction matters and deserves your full attention.

The first thing to understand about Japanese convenience stores — konbini — is that the food is genuinely good. Not 'good for a convenience store' good. Just good. The onigiri at 7-Eleven is made fresh every morning. The egg salad sandwich at Lawson is produced with a specific grade of bread and a specific ratio of egg to mayonnaise that has been optimized through decades of iteration. The hot foods at Family Mart are cooked to a standard that most fast-casual restaurants in other countries don't meet.

Konbini are everywhere. In Tokyo, you're never more than a three-minute walk from one. They're open 24 hours. They sell alcohol until midnight or all night depending on the prefecture. They have ATMs that accept foreign cards — the 7-Bank machines at 7-Eleven are the most consistently reliable. They sell stamps, ship packages, pay utility bills, and print documents from a USB drive. They are infrastructure, and understanding them is understanding how Japan works.

What to eat. Onigiri are rice balls with a filling — tuna mayo, pickled plum, salmon, bonito — wrapped in a three-panel system that keeps the seaweed crispy until you open it. The opening system is printed on the packet with numbered arrows. Follow them. Don't try to unwrap it another way. You will end up with rice everywhere and no dignified explanation.

The egg salad sandwich (tamago sando) at 7-Eleven specifically has developed a following that crosses national lines. The egg is thick-cut and slightly sweet. The bread is soft in the particular way that soft white bread in Japan is soft, which is different from anywhere else and difficult to explain. Buy one on your first morning and the question of why people travel to Japan specifically for the food will answer itself.

Hot food: the oden pot is a winter staple — simmering broth with fish cakes, tofu, daikon, and boiled eggs. You pick items with tongs and the staff counts them into your bill. Steamed buns (nikuman) live in the hot case near the register and are excellent at 11pm when nothing else is open. The Family Mart chicken has been the subject of genuine national debate about whether it surpasses McDonald's. The answer depends on the outlet and the day.

Drinks: canned coffee in Japan is a separate category of human achievement. Boss Coffee and the konbini own-brand cans have been developed through R&D cycles that most car companies would find excessive. Around ¥120 a can, available cold or hot depending on the season. The hot canned coffee on a November morning in Tokyo is one of those sensory memories that travel writers try to describe and never quite manage.

Practical notes: budget ¥500–700 for a full konbini meal. Pay at the register. Staff will ask 'atatame masuka?' (would you like this warmed up?) for eligible items. Yes is the answer. There will also be a question about a bag — a small fee applies, but it's worth it. Don't eat inside the store if it can be avoided; there's usually a counter or seats outside.
