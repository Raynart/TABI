Koenji and Shimokitazawa: Tokyo for People Who've Already Done Harajuku

Two stops on different lines. Two neighborhoods that run on thrift stores, live music, and a specific refusal to be improved.

In this article

  1. What these neighborhoods are
  2. Koenji: the vintage ecosystem
  3. Koenji: bars and izakaya
  4. Shimokitazawa: live music
  5. The Sunday market

What these neighborhoods are

Harajuku is famous. Shimokitazawa is known. Koenji is where people who've lived in Tokyo for five years go when they want to be reminded what the city felt like before everything got expensive and curated. Both Koenji and Shimokitazawa deserve an afternoon each, and the fastest way to understand why is to walk from the station in either direction without a destination.

Koenji: the vintage ecosystem

Koenji, on the Chuo Line, is Tokyo's vintage district — not vintage-as-aesthetic but vintage-as-ecosystem. The streets around the north and south exits of the station contain somewhere between 50 and 80 second-hand clothing shops, each specializing in something different: one does only 70s Western denim, another specializes in military surplus, a third has nothing but vintage sportswear priced by the kilo. The Awa Odori festival in August draws around 300,000 people to this neighborhood specifically, making it briefly the loudest and most populated neighborhood in all of Tokyo.

Koenji: bars and izakaya

The coffee shops and bars in Koenji are a separate argument for visiting. Pure, a jazz bar in continuous operation since the 1980s, plays vinyl records on equipment that hasn't changed since opening. There are izakaya here that predate the bubble economy and have no interest in attracting anyone who found them on an app. The food is good and cheap: a tonkatsu set lunch is ¥950, a yakitori skewer is ¥120.

Shimokitazawa: live music

Shimokitazawa, accessible via the Odakyu Line from Shinjuku or Keio Inokashira from Shibuya, has a different character. It's the live music neighborhood: more than 30 small music venues (livehouse) are within a 10-minute walk of the station, ranging from 50-person basement rooms to 500-capacity halls. Any night of the week, most of them have shows. Tickets are typically ¥2,000–3,000 at the door.

The Sunday market

The Sunday market at Shimokitazawa draws vintage sellers, plant vendors, record collectors, and food stalls to the streets outside the station. It's a neighborhood event rather than a tourist attraction, and has the energy of something that's been happening every week for years and has no interest in being photographed for social media. Show up around 11am with no plan and spend two hours. That's the correct approach to Shimokitazawa.
