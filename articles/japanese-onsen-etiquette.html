The Onsen Rules Nobody Bothers to Tell You

The water is shared. The silence is expected. The tattoo policy is more complicated than you've been told.

In this article

  1. The rule everyone needs to know
  2. What you bring in
  3. Tattoos: the real policy
  4. Temperature
  5. Rotating between baths
  6. After the onsen

The rule everyone needs to know

The fundamental rule of a Japanese onsen or public bath is that you wash before you enter the communal water. This is not optional and not a suggestion. There are shower stations around the perimeter 窶・a stool, a handheld showerhead, shampoo and conditioner in dispensers. You sit on the stool, rinse yourself thoroughly, wash your hair if you're going in with your head back, and then you may enter the bath. Anyone who walks past the shower stations and steps directly into the communal water has misunderstood what they're doing here.

What you bring in

What you bring into the bath: nothing, essentially. A small towel is acceptable if kept out of the water 窶・fold it and put it on your head, or leave it at the edge of the tub. Soap goes at the shower station, not into the bath. Your phone doesn't come into the onsen area at all. This is enforced in most facilities and enforced seriously.

Tattoos: the real policy

Tattoos: the official policy at most traditional onsen is no tattoos, a rule that dates to historical associations between tattoos and organized crime. Enforcement varies significantly. Large resort onsen tend to be stricter; smaller neighborhood sento (public baths) are often more pragmatic. Private family baths (kashikiri buro), bookable by the hour at many ryokan and onsen hotels, have no policy because there are no other guests. If you have visible tattoos and want to use a communal onsen without anxiety, look for facilities with explicit tattoo-friendly policies 窶・they exist and are increasingly common.

Temperature

Temperature: onsen run hot. 40窶・2ﾂｰC is standard. 44ﾂｰC exists. The first immersion will feel excessive. Give yourself 30 seconds to acclimatize before concluding you can't handle it. The correct approach is to enter slowly, sit still, and breathe steadily. You will feel fine within a minute. If you feel dizzy or lightheaded at any point, get out. This is normal and the fault of the heat, not weakness.

Rotating between baths

Rotating baths and timing: most onsen have multiple baths 窶・an indoor main bath, possibly an outdoor rotenburo, a cold plunge bath, a sauna, a carbonated bath. The protocol is to move between them over 30窶・0 minutes rather than sitting in one until you've cooked. Cold plunge (mizu buro) after a hot soak is optional but converts most people the first time they try it. The contrast is the point.

After the onsen

After the bath: dry off in the changing room before going anywhere else. The post-onsen state 窶・warm, slightly light-headed, skin strangely soft 窶・is best enjoyed horizontal for 15 minutes. Drink water. Many ryokan leave cold water or barley tea in the changing room for this purpose. The evening onsen before bed followed by eight hours of sleep in a cool room is, empirically, the best sleep you will have all year.
