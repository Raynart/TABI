What to Buy When You Buy Japanese Sake

Sake is rice wine in the same way that champagne is grape juice. The category is more complex than most people expect, and the range of quality is enormous.

The two main variables

Sake is fermented rice, water, yeast, and koji mold. The main variables: how much the rice has been polished before brewing (the seimaibuai, expressed as a percentage of the grain remaining), and whether distilled alcohol has been added. These two factors create the main classification categories that appear on labels and that are worth understanding before you buy anything.

Classifications that matter

The classifications that matter. Junmai: pure rice sake, no added alcohol. Honjozo: a small amount of distilled alcohol added, which lightens the sake and adds aroma. Ginjo: rice polished to at least 60% (40% milled away), more aromatic. Daiginjo: polished to at least 50%, the top tier, typically the most delicate and expensive. Combine these: Junmai Daiginjo is pure rice, top-tier polish, usually the premium product. Futsushu is table sake — no premium designation, reasonable for everyday drinking.

What to actually buy

What to actually buy. For a gift or something to drink on the trip: a Junmai Ginjo or Junmai Daiginjo from a mid-sized regional brewery priced between ¥2,000 and ¥5,000 (720ml). The big names (Dassai, Kubota, Hakkaisan) are reliably good and widely available in department stores and specialist shops; they became famous because they're excellent. Dassai 23 (polished to 23% remaining) is exceptional and expensive (around ¥5,000 for 300ml); Dassai 39 is similar in character at two-thirds the price.

Nigori sake

Nigori sake (unfiltered, cloudy, with rice sediment) is best drunk cold and fresh — it doesn't travel as well. If you're drinking in Japan, try it; if you're buying to bring home, stick to clear filtered sake in sealed bottles.

Where to buy

Where to buy: department store basement floors (depachika) have the best curated range and staff who can explain what they're selling. Specialist sake shops (sakaya) exist in most cities — Tokyo's Hasegawa Saketen chain is a reliable option with English-speaking staff at some locations. Avoid convenience store sake for gifts; it's functional but unmemorable.

Storage and shelf life

Storage and shelf life: premium sake is best kept refrigerated and consumed within a few months of purchase. If you're transporting it home, keep it cold where possible and drink it within six months. Nigori in particular degrades faster. Honjozo and Futsushu are more forgiving. Buy what you can drink.
