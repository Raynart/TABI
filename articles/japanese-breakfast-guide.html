The Japanese Breakfast: What It Is and Why It Will Change Your Morning

A traditional Japanese breakfast is not a quick meal. It is a composed sequence of small things done well, and eating it once will make every hotel breakfast you eat for the rest of your life feel like a concession.

In this article

  1. What a Japanese breakfast is
  2. The grilled fish
  3. Miso soup
  4. Where to eat one
  5. What it teaches you

What a Japanese breakfast is

A traditional Japanese breakfast (washoku asa-gohan) consists of: steamed white rice, miso soup (with tofu, wakame, and green onion), a piece of grilled fish (typically salmon, mackerel, or sanma), a small selection of pickled vegetables (tsukemono), a square of tofu, a small egg dish (tamagoyaki or onsen tamago), and nori (dried seaweed). Everything arrives simultaneously. Nothing is sweet. Coffee is available separately, usually afterward. This is the standard morning meal across ryokan, traditional hotels, and the home kitchens of people who cook breakfast.

The grilled fish

The grilled fish is the anchor. A properly grilled piece of sake (salmon) or saba (mackerel) 窶・salted and grilled slowly over charcoal or in a specialized fish broiler 窶・has a crisp skin, slightly smoky interior, and a flavor that is to a hotel breakfast buffet as a proper espresso is to an office coffee machine. At a ryokan, this fish was probably grilled minutes before it arrived. Eat it immediately.

Miso soup

Miso soup at a proper breakfast is made from dashi (usually kombu and katsuobushi stock) and miso paste, not from a powder. The difference is apparent. The soup arrives hot in a lacquerware bowl with a lid. Lift the lid, place it on the side inverted, drink directly from the bowl, or use the small spoon provided. Pouring the miso soup over your rice is acceptable in informal settings. Mixing the rice with everything into a kind of porridge 窶・ochazuke-style, though that's technically green tea poured over rice 窶・is a personal approach that some people find comforting.

Where to eat one

The best Japanese breakfast is the one at a ryokan, where it arrives in your room or a dedicated dining room and takes 45 minutes to eat properly. The second-best is at a traditional Japanese hotel (a business hotel that has opted to serve Japanese breakfast rather than a buffet). The kissaten morning set 窶・coffee, toast, sometimes a small egg 窶・is a different, simpler thing entirely. The convenience store breakfast is useful and fast and not the same experience.

What it teaches you

Eating a Japanese breakfast once 窶・properly, without hurrying 窶・resets certain assumptions about what mornings can be. The meal is light enough that you feel alert afterward rather than heavy. The flavors are savory and complex at 7am in a way that feels unusual until it feels obvious. The effort that went into it is visible in every dish. For many visitors, the ryokan breakfast is the meal they remember longest from the trip. Arrive at the dining room at the scheduled time, sit down properly, and give it the 45 minutes it is asking for.
