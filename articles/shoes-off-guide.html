When to Take Your Shoes Off in Japan: The Complete Guide

The shoe rule in Japan is not arbitrary. It reflects a clear line between outside space and inside space that structures daily life. Here is where that line is drawn.

The genkan

You step into the genkan, take off your shoes, and step up into the interior in socks or bare feet. At some temples

Certain temple halls require shoes to be removed before entering. There will be a step at the entrance and either a rack for shoes or plastic bags to carry them inside with you. This varies by temple. Fushimi Inari in Kyoto does not require shoe removal for the main torii paths. Todai-ji in Nara does for the main hall interior. The entrance step is the consistent signal.

The toilet slipper trap

The most reliable mistake visitors make: walking out of the toilet in the toilet slippers. At ryokan and some traditional homes, there is a dedicated pair of slippers inside the toilet room. You put them on to enter, and you take them off when you leave. Walking back through the inn in toilet slippers is the equivalent of walking through the house carrying the toilet brush. It happens to almost everyone once. The correction is to look down at your feet before exiting the toilet.

Reading the signals

The consistent signal that shoes come off: a step at the entrance that marks a change in floor level. When you see a step, assume shoes off until you have evidence otherwise. Modern hotels, convenience stores, restaurants with Western seating, and most commercial buildings do not follow this rule. The genkan-step is the dividing line.
