The IC Card Is the Best Thing in Japan. Here's How to Actually Use It.

You can survive Tokyo's train network without one. You just won't want to.

Before you leave the airport, find a ticket machine and get a Suica. Don't deliberate. Don't check whether you really need one. You need one.

An IC card is a rechargeable transit card — the same basic technology as London's Oyster or Hong Kong's Octopus — except that in Japan it works on practically everything. Trains, subways, buses, ferries, taxis in major cities, convenience stores, vending machines, coin lockers, the self-checkout at Don Quijote at 2am. The list keeps growing.

Suica is issued by JR East and works in Tokyo and most of eastern Japan. ICOCA is the Kansai equivalent, issued by JR West, and works in Osaka, Kyoto, Kobe, and surrounding areas. The practical answer to which one you should get: they're fully interoperable. Get whichever one is available at the airport you land at. It genuinely doesn't matter.

Loading money: find any green JR ticket machine, insert your card, press 'Charge' (there's an English option), and add ¥1,000, ¥2,000, or ¥5,000. Keep ¥2,000 or more on the card at all times. You don't want to be the person topping up at the fare gate while a queue builds behind you.

Tapping is the ritual that will define your first week. Tap the card on the reader as you enter the gate — a satisfying beep. Tap again as you leave — another beep, money deducted. A sad double-beep and a red light means insufficient balance. Step aside, top up at the nearest machine, which will be approximately five meters away.

One thing nobody mentions: the card works in vending machines too. Just tap the reader on the front panel. It's faster than coins, works every time, and makes carrying change feel archaic. After a week in Japan you'll find physical coins faintly offensive.

Increasingly, you can also link Suica to Apple Pay or Google Pay. Setup takes about ten minutes and is worth doing if your phone supports it — you can top up from the app, which means you never need to find a machine. The setup process is in English on both platforms.

When you leave Japan: keep the card. It doesn't expire, and having it ready for your next trip is genuinely convenient. If you want to return it, do so at a JR ticket office — you'll get back your remaining balance minus a ¥220 handling fee. If you have less than ¥300 left, just keep it.

The larger point is this: the IC card makes it possible to navigate Tokyo's 130-plus stations within the Yamanote Line loop without ever buying a specific-destination ticket from a machine. That is not a trivial thing. It removes a class of friction that makes many transit systems — especially large Japanese ones — intimidating for first-time visitors. Get the card at the airport. Everything else is easier.
