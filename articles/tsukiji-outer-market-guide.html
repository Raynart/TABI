Tsukiji Outer Market: Where to Actually Eat (and What to Skip)

The inner market moved to Toyosu in 2018. The outer market never moved and never needed to.

  2. When to arrive
  3. Sushi

What stayed and what moved

In 2018, the famous wholesale fish market at Tsukiji relocated operations to Toyosu, a reclaimed island further east. Travel writers immediately started writing elegies for Tsukiji. What they missed is that the part most visitors cared about — the outer market, with its 400-odd shops selling fresh seafood, street food, knives, and tamago-yaki — never went anywhere and shows no signs of going anywhere.

When to arrive

The outer market opens early. Most stalls are running by 6am. The target arrival window is between 7am and 10am, when the selection is widest and the food is freshest. By noon, the best stalls will have sold out of the things worth eating. Come for breakfast. This is not negotiable.

Sushi

What to eat, in order of importance. First: sushi from one of the small counters in the side streets off the main market lane. These are eight-seat affairs with a glass case of fish in front. Point at what you want, sit down, eat it immediately. The fish was at Toyosu wholesale market this morning. A ¥1,500–2,000 set will be the best sushi you've had in your life, and if you don't believe that until you're eating it, that's fine.

Tamagoyaki

Second: tamagoyaki, the thick-rolled sweet omelette that is specific to the Tsukiji style. The most famous vendors are Tamagoyaki-ya Shouro and the stalls along the Namiyoke Shrine side. They sell slices from the pan on wooden sticks. The outside is slightly caramelized. The inside is custard-soft. It costs around ¥200 and you will want two.

Ikura on rice

Third: ikura (salmon roe) on rice, from any stall that makes it fresh. The roe should be bright orange and very fresh — the texture is different from what you've likely had before. A small bowl is ¥500–800. It's one of those things that recalibrates your understanding of what fish can be.

What to skip

What to skip: the photographed seafood-on-skewer vendors near the main entrance that have been optimized for social media. The scallops are fine but priced for tourists, and the line is full of people taking the same photo. Walk past them, turn into the side streets, and find the stalls where nobody has their phone out.

The knife shops

The knife shops deserve a separate mention. Tsukiji has more professional knife dealers per square meter than anywhere outside of the knife-making cities. If you're planning to buy Japanese kitchen knives — and you should, they are among the best things to bring home from Japan — buy them here after handling them and talking to the vendor. The selection goes from ¥3,000 santoku knives for everyday home cooking to ¥80,000 custom yanagiba for the serious cook.

Getting there

Getting there: subway to Tsukijishijo (Oedo Line) or Tsukiji (Hibiya Line). Allow at least two hours. Three if you're serious about the knives.
