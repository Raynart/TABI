What You Can and Cannot Photograph in Japan

Japan is exceptionally photogenic. It also has specific places and situations where photography is restricted, disrespectful, or now actively prohibited. Knowing the difference matters.

In this article

  1. The general principle
  2. Inside temples and shrines
  3. At onsen and public baths
  4. Photographing strangers
  5. In Gion, Kyoto
  6. On trains

The general principle

In most outdoor public spaces in Japan, photography is unrestricted. Streets, parks, temple grounds, shrine pathways, market areas, train stations 遯ｶ繝ｻall are freely photographable. The restrictions apply inside specific buildings, in areas where signage prohibits it, and in situations where the photography causes problems for other people or for the place itself. Read the signs, watch what other people are doing, and when in doubt, ask.

Inside temples and shrines

Interior photography inside the main hall of a temple or shrine is usually prohibited. The sign will typically show a camera with a red line through it. Some places allow photography inside with a fee; some famous main halls (Kinkaku-ji遯ｶ蜀ｱ interior, Todai-ji遯ｶ蜀ｱ Buddha) are not accessible at all. Gardens attached to temples are generally freely photographable. Detailed garden photography is expected 遯ｶ繝ｻit遯ｶ蜀ｱ one of the primary reasons people visit.

At onsen and public baths

Photography is prohibited at onsen and sento (public baths), including in the changing rooms. This is absolute and enforced. Phones are not permitted in the bathing areas of most facilities, and taking your phone into a bathing area is both a rule violation and a privacy invasion of every other person present. Leave the phone in the locker.

Photographing strangers

Japan doesn遯ｶ蜀ｲ have a specific law against street photography, but the social norms around photographing strangers are similar to those in most countries: pointing a camera directly at someone遯ｶ蜀ｱ face without consent, following someone for a photograph, or photographing children without parental consent all cross reasonable lines. Most Japanese people will not object verbally, but this reflects a cultural reluctance to confront rather than permission.

In Gion, Kyoto

Gion and the surrounding geisha districts of Kyoto are a specific and documented problem. Years of tourists surrounding and following maiko and geiko for photographs has led to complaints from residents, businesses, and the performers themselves. Some streets now have photography restriction signs during evening hours. Obeying these is not optional 遯ｶ繝ｻlocal authorities have the ability to issue fines. More simply: if you see a maiko walking to an appointment, she is not a photo opportunity. She is a person doing her job.

On trains

Photography on trains is not explicitly prohibited but is not commonly practiced for good reason: photographing other passengers without consent in an enclosed space they cannot leave is uncomfortable regardless of country. Photographing the view from the window is universally fine. Photographing your own group is fine. Pointing a camera at strangers in a carriage is not.
