How to Order and Drink Sake in Japan Without Getting It Wrong

Sake is Japan's most misunderstood drink. Most people drink it too warm, in the wrong glass, with the wrong food. This is how to do it correctly.

In this article

  1. Sake is not always drunk hot
  2. You pour for others, not yourself
  3. The receiving gesture
  4. What to order
  5. Kanpai

Sake is not always drunk hot

The most persistent misunderstanding about sake outside Japan is that it is drunk hot. Sake is drunk at a range of temperatures 窶・cold (reishu), room temperature (jouon), slightly warm (nuru-kan), hot (atsu-kan) 窶・and the temperature used depends on the type of sake and personal preference. Premium ginjo and daiginjo sake are almost always drunk cold or at room temperature; heating them diminishes the delicate aromatics that justify their price. Warmer temperatures suit rougher, more robust honjozo or futsushu. At a good sake restaurant, asking which temperature is recommended for a specific bottle is a reasonable question and will get a thoughtful answer.

You pour for others, not yourself

The social convention around sake at a table is that you pour for others and they pour for you. You do not fill your own cup. If someone's cup is empty and you have the carafe, pour for them. If your cup is empty, hold it up slightly toward whoever has the carafe 窶・this is the signal. At a very informal dinner with close friends this rule is relaxed; at any formal or semi-formal context it applies. Pouring for yourself first is read as either self-centered or unaware of the convention.

The receiving gesture

When someone pours sake for you, you do not just hold the cup. You hold it with both hands, or support it from below with one hand while holding it with the other. This is the equivalent of receiving a gift with both hands 窶・a gesture that says you are receiving attentively. When the pour is done, you drink (or set the cup down), and then you pour in return.

What to order

At an izakaya, the standard entry point is a carafe (tokkuri) rather than a bottle. Carafes come in small (1 go, about 180ml) and large (2 go, 360ml) sizes. The small carafe is the right starting order while you are deciding. For a meal at a sake specialist or a good Japanese restaurant, the menu will have sake listed with classifications (junmai, ginjo, daiginjo). Ask for osusume (what do you recommend) and specify whether you prefer lighter or richer, fruity or dry. The staff will know.

Kanpai

The toast is kanpai. Eyes contact, glasses meet lightly (for sake cups, tap gently 窶・they are fragile), drink at the same time. It is customary to wait for kanpai before drinking, even if the sake arrives before everyone is served. Making eye contact during kanpai is not optional 窶・it is the point of the gesture. Looking away while toasting is the universal bad-omen equivalent and works the same in Japan as anywhere else.
