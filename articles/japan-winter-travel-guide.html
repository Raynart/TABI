Why January Is the Best Month to Visit Japan (Yes, Really)

No cherry blossoms. No 38-degree humidity. No crowds. January is when Japan stops performing for tourists and just exists.

Why even consider January

Summer in Japan is spectacular and exhausting. Cherry blossom season is magical and so crowded that the famous spots feel like theme parks. Autumn foliage is genuinely among the most beautiful things in the natural world and is also, by October, completely overrun. January has none of these problems, because January has a reputation for cold and grey that is only partly deserved.

The actual weather

The actual weather in Tokyo in January: between 4ﾂｰC and 10ﾂｰC during the day, clear skies more often than you'd expect, occasional rain, rare snow. Not harsh. A good wool coat is enough. Kyoto gets occasional snow 窶・which on temple grounds with grey stones and winter moss looks exactly like the photograph you've seen your whole life, except there are almost no other people in it.

Where the crowds went

What January removes: the tourist surge disappears after New Year's (January 1窶・), when domestic travelers return home and the holiday-season international visitors are gone. From January 5th onwards, you can visit Kinkaku-ji without being engulfed in 2,000 people. You can walk the Arashiyama bamboo grove with no one in your frame. This is not an exaggeration. On a cold January Tuesday, the path is genuinely empty.

New Year rituals

What January adds: the rituals of New Year's 窶・hatsumode, the first shrine visit of the year 窶・give the first week of January a feeling of purposefulness that ordinary months don't have. Major shrines are packed January 1窶・ and quieter but still festive through the first week. Asakusa's Senso-ji at 6am on January 2nd is one of the best crowds in the world to be part of.

Onsen towns in winter

Onsen towns are at their best in winter, and this is not a small thing. The specific pleasure of sitting in an outdoor rotenburo 窶・a hot spring bath 窶・with snow falling and steam rising is not available in July. Hakone from Tokyo, Kinosaki Onsen from Kyoto, Beppu in Kyushu: these are all better in winter on nearly every metric. The baths are hotter, the air is colder, and the bowl of food you eat afterwards is twice as satisfying.

Skiing in Hokkaido

Ski resorts: Japan has some of the best powder snow in the world. Hokkaido specifically 窶・Niseko, Furano, Rusutsu 窶・has developed into a serious international ski destination, partly because the light dry snow that falls there is genuinely exceptional. January is peak season. If skiing is relevant to your trip, January in Hokkaido is not a consolation prize. It's the main event.

Flights, hotels, and prices

The practical case: flights cost less in January than in October or April. Hotels cost less. Popular restaurants with months-long waiting lists have more availability. The ryokan in Hakone that has no rooms in November has rooms in January, often at a meaningfully lower rate.

What winter reveals

What Japan offers in winter that it can't offer in spring or autumn: something closer to daily life. When a place is over-visited, it optimizes for visitors. When it's not being watched, it relaxes. January Japan is commuters and schoolchildren and the ramen shop that opens at 6:30am for the people who work early shifts. That Japan is worth seeing, and it's more accessible than people think.
