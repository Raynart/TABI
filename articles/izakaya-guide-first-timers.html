How to Eat at an Izakaya Without Looking Completely Lost

The curtain over the door. The laminated menu with pictures on maybe two pages. The small charge you didn't order. You'll be fine.

What an izakaya is

An izakaya is a Japanese pub 窶・not a restaurant, not a bar, but the thing that happens when the two concepts are combined without either one winning. You drink and eat in roughly equal measure. The food comes in small plates meant to be shared across the table. Nobody orders a main course. The lighting is low and the room is loud and you'll probably be sharing it with salarymen who have loosened their ties, which is always a good sign.

Ordering from the menu

Most izakaya have a printed menu and sometimes a laminated picture menu. Order from the picture menu if there is one. Point at what you want. Nobody will be offended. If there's no picture menu, say 'osusume wa nan desu ka?' 窶・what do you recommend? Someone will point at something, and whatever they point at will be edible.

The opening round

The standard opening order: a round of beers (nama biru, draft, or a sawa cocktail made from shochu and soda), plus two or three small dishes to start. Edamame, karaage (fried chicken), and potato salad are almost universally available and almost universally good. They are not exciting. They are correct. Order them while you figure out the rest of the menu.

Otoshi: the charge you did not order

The small charge that appears on your bill without explanation: it's called otoshi. Almost every izakaya charges a per-person seating fee of ﾂ･200窶・00, and in return brings a small snack you didn't order 窶・sometimes pickled vegetables, sometimes a small salad, occasionally something genuinely excellent. It's not a scam. It's standard. The bill is correct.

What to eat

Order drinks first, then food, then more drinks, then more food 窶・loosely. Good things to order beyond the basics: yakitori (grilled chicken on bamboo skewers 窶・order mixed, specify 'tare' for sweet soy glaze or 'shio' for salt), dashimaki tamago (rolled omelette made with dashi broth, usually soft and fragrant), and sashimi if the place looks like it takes its fish seriously, which you can usually tell from whether there are locals at the bar eating it.

Shochu and chuhai

Shochu is likely what you're drinking in the cocktails and don't know it. Distilled most commonly from sweet potato (imo-jochu) or barley (mugi-jochu), it's served over ice or mixed with soda. Gentler than whisky, it goes with everything on the menu. If someone offers chuhai, that's shochu with fruit soda 窶・the izakaya equivalent of a spritz, and perfectly fine.

Getting the bill

You leave when you've decided to leave. To get the bill, catch someone's eye and make a rectangular gesture with your index fingers 窶・this is the universal 'o-kaikei' signal and everyone will understand it immediately. The total will likely be split evenly if you're with a group. This is called warikan. Don't try to calculate individual shares. It goes against the spirit of the thing.

The golden rule

The golden rule: the more local and less translated the menu, the better the food. The izakaya in a residential neighborhood near a train station that isn't on any tourist map will beat the one with an English sign near the temples every time. Your hotel is on the tourist circuit. The izakaya should not be.
