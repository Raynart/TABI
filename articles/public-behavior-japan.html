How to Behave in Public in Japan: The Unwritten Rules

Don窶冲 eat while walking

Eating while walking is generally not done in Japan. The exception is festival food from stalls at a matsuri (festival), where the eating-while-standing context is explicit. The rule applies elsewhere: you finish your konbini onigiri standing outside the shop, not while walking down the street. Street food vendors at tourist sites have started providing small eating areas specifically because this norm exists and tourists don窶冲 know it. Use them.

Queuing is not optional

Japan queues. It queues at ATMs, at crosswalks, at train doors, at ramen shops, at theme park rides. Cutting in line is an extremely unwelcome behavior. If there is no visible queue but a crowd, look for the end of it and join there. Lines at Japanese institutions are remarkably orderly and self-policing 窶・no one will say anything if you push in, but everyone will notice, and the social discomfort this creates for you is real and immediate.

Noise levels

Japan is quieter than most countries in public spaces. Not silent 窶・Shinjuku at 6pm is genuinely loud 窶・but the background noise level in trains, restaurants, and streets comes from the environment rather than individual conversations. Speaking at a volume that carries beyond your table in a restaurant, laughing loudly in a way that commands the room窶冱 attention, or holding a speakerphone conversation in a convenience store are all behaviors that will cause people to move away from you. This is the social feedback system.

Escalators

On escalators, the standing side is the left. The right side is for walking. This is the Tokyo convention; Osaka does it in reverse (stand right, walk left) and this causes visible confusion at every point where Tokyoites and Osakans intersect. When in doubt, follow the person in front of you. The escalator rule is enforced entirely by social pressure and the occasional polite request to stand to one side, but it is real and consistent.

There are no rubbish bins

Japan removed most public rubbish bins following a 1995 sarin gas attack. This means that in most public spaces 窶・streets, parks, station platforms 窶・there is nowhere to throw away your waste. You carry it with you until you reach a convenience store (which has a bin near the register), a restaurant, or your accommodation. Konbini is the practical answer for most of the day. This is not as inconvenient as it sounds once you accept that the rubbish is coming with you.

Photography of strangers

Photographing people without consent in Japan operates under the same social norms as most countries, but with one additional consideration: the geisha districts of Kyoto (particularly Gion) have become sites of harassment as visitors photograph geiko and maiko at close range. Some streets in Gion now restrict photography entirely during certain hours. Photographing someone in traditional dress without asking, following them for a shot, or blocking a path for a photograph are all behaviors that have become serious enough to prompt legal responses in some areas. In all other contexts: use judgment, ask when in doubt, and take no for an answer.
