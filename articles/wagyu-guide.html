How to Actually Eat Wagyu Beef in Japan

Wagyu is expensive. This is not a misunderstanding. The question is what you're getting for the expense, and whether a ﾂ･5,000 lunch version is the same as a ﾂ･30,000 dinner.

  2. Kobe, Matsusaka, and Omi
  3. Formats and prices
  4. The accessible option
  5. The one thing worth knowing

What wagyu actually is

Wagyu refers to four specific Japanese beef cattle breeds 窶・Black, Brown, Shorthorn, and Polled 窶・of which Japanese Black (Kuroge Wagashu) is the one you'll encounter at restaurants. The defining characteristic is intramuscular fat (shimofuri), distributed through the muscle in a marbling pattern that creates the butter-soft texture wagyu is known for. The marbling is graded A1 to A5 by the Japan Meat Grading Association; A5 is the maximum.

Kobe, Matsusaka, and Omi

The three regions you'll encounter most often. Kobe beef (from Tajima-strain cattle in Hyogo Prefecture): the most famous internationally, and genuinely excellent, though supply is far smaller than the global market for 'Kobe beef' implies 窶・most 'Kobe beef' outside Japan is not from Kobe. Matsusaka beef from Mie Prefecture is considered by many Japanese food professionals to be superior in fat quality; it's less famous internationally because nobody started a marketing campaign for it in the 1990s. Omi beef from Shiga, one of the oldest beef brands in Japan, is the option for people who've read enough about wagyu to want the historical footnote.

Formats and prices

Formats and pricing. A5 wagyu as yakiniku (grilled at the table over charcoal) is the most common restaurant format 窶・ﾂ･3,000窶・,000 for a plate of premium cuts, best shared. A5 wagyu sukiyaki (simmered in a sweet soy broth with tofu and vegetables) is the winter version and is slightly more forgiving for first-timers because the broth moderates the richness. Wagyu as a steak, Western-style, is available in department store restaurants and high-end steakhouses 窶・expect ﾂ･15,000窶・0,000 per person for the premium experience.

The accessible option

The practical option: department store basement food halls (depachika) in Tokyo, Osaka, and Kyoto sell wagyu by the slice for home cooking and often have prepared wagyu bentos and small yakiniku sets. A high-quality wagyu beef bowl (gyudon) at a specialist restaurant like Yoshida Gyu in Tokyo can be had for ﾂ･1,500窶・,000 and gives a genuine sense of what A4窶鄭5 wagyu tastes like without the full restaurant-dinner commitment.

The one thing worth knowing

The one thing worth knowing: wagyu at A5 is rich. A small amount satisfies in a way that lean beef doesn't. At a proper yakiniku restaurant, a 100g portion is enough for one person. The instinct to order more than you can eat is strong and almost always incorrect. Order one plate, taste it properly, and then decide if you want another.
