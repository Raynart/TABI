Silent mode means silent

The first thing Japanese train operators ask is that you set your phone to manner mode (silent). This means no ringtone, no notification sounds, no speaker audio of any kind. It does not mean vibrate. In a quiet carriage, a buzzing phone on a hard surface is clearly audible to the six people nearest you. Put it fully silent. This takes two seconds and prevents one of the more uncomfortable social experiences available to a visitor.

Phone calls are not done

Taking a phone call on a Japanese commuter train is a social violation roughly equivalent to eating a sandwich in a library. The standard approach is to let it go to voicemail, or to answer, say quietly that you are on a train and will call back, and end the call immediately. This is what Japanese commuters do. On long-distance Shinkansen, calls are generally acceptable in the space between carriages. In the carriage itself, the same rules apply.

Priority seats

The seats marked with a priority label near the carriage doors (often in a different color or fabric) are reserved for elderly passengers, pregnant women, passengers with young children, and those with injuries or disabilities. On a crowded train, these seats fill up like any other. On a less crowded train, the norm is to leave them empty unless you are one of the intended users. The nuance: at 11pm on a Friday, nobody enforces this strictly. At 8am on a Monday, a healthy young person sitting in a priority seat while an elderly passenger stands nearby is making a choice that other passengers will notice.

Platform queuing

Most Tokyo platforms have painted lines on the ground showing where train doors will open and which direction passengers should queue. You queue behind the lines, in two parallel columns, and you let people off the train before you board. This is not complicated, but it is enforced by social pressure rather than barriers, and it fails immediately when visitors don遯ｶ蜀ｲ understand the system. Look at the floor. Find the lines. Stand in them.

Bags and backpacks

On a crowded train, a backpack worn on your back occupies the space of an additional person and hits other passengers every time you turn. The protocol is to take it off and either hold it in front of you or place it in the overhead luggage shelf. Large rolling suitcases go in the space at the end of the carriage near the doors, not in the aisle. This is an issue specifically at peak hours on the Narita Express and on Shinkansen platforms during tourist seasons.
