The rule

Do not tip in Japan. Not at restaurants. Not in taxis. Not at the ryokan. Not at the hotel. The rule applies everywhere, with essentially no exceptions. This is not a matter of the tip being too small or the service not meriting it. Tipping is simply not part of the service culture, and attempting it creates confusion rather than goodwill.

Why tipping is not done

In Japan, service is understood as part of what you paid for. The waiter who refills your water, the taxi driver who opens and closes the door automatically from the front seat, the ryokan attendant who arranges your futon 遯ｶ繝ｻthey are doing their job with the full understanding that it will be done to a high standard because that is the expectation, not because there is a financial incentive to exceed it. Tipping implies that the service was beyond what was expected, which suggests the expectation was low. This is not the message most people intend to send.

What happens if you try

If you leave cash on the table at a restaurant, staff will often chase you down the street to return it, assuming you forgot it. If you hand money directly to a server as a tip, it is likely to be declined, or accepted with visible discomfort. In rare cases it may be accepted with genuine bewilderment. None of these outcomes is the one you were hoping for.

An o-shibori (a small envelope with a gratuity inside) can be given to the ryokan attendant assigned to your room on arrival, as a gesture of appreciation for the personal service throughout the stay. The envelope matters as much as the money.
