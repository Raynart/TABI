Kanazawa: The City Most Japan Itineraries Skip 窶・and Shouldn't

Three hours from Tokyo by bullet train. A fraction of Kyoto's crowds. Arguably better seafood. There is no good reason it's on so few itineraries.

Why Kanazawa is overlooked

Kanazawa is on the wrong side of Japan. That seems to be the only explanation for why it receives a third of Kyoto's visitor numbers while having a castle, a geisha district, one of Japan's three great landscape gardens, and what most serious food people consider the best seafood market outside of Tokyo.

Getting there

The bullet train from Tokyo (the Hokuriku Shinkansen, extended in 2015) takes 2.5 hours. From Kyoto it's 2.5 to 3 hours with a transfer. Neither journey is difficult. The problem is psychological 窶・most visitors arrive with a Tokyo-Kyoto-Osaka triangle already built into their itinerary, and Kanazawa sits outside of it, requiring a detour that most itineraries can't absorb.

Kenroku-en

Kenroku-en is the main draw, and correctly so. One of Japan's three great gardens 窶・a designation given by Edo-period landscape critics 窶・it was developed over two centuries by the ruling Maeda clan and covers 11.4 hectares. In cherry blossom season it's crowded. In late November, when the pine trees are wrapped in rope against the winter snow in a technique called yukizuri, it's extraordinary and very nearly empty.

Higashi Chaya

Higashi Chaya is Kanazawa's best-preserved geisha quarter and is smaller and less tourist-managed than Kyoto's Gion. Some of the ochaya (teahouses) are still operating. The street is wooden and narrow and hasn't changed structurally since the early 19th century. Come early morning or at dusk and it's yours.

The food

The food. Kanazawa sits on the Sea of Japan coast and receives fish from waters that don't reach the Pacific side in the same condition. The seafood at Omicho Market 窶・a covered market the size of a small neighborhood 窶・is some of the best in the country. In winter: crab, specifically the male snow crab called zuwaigani. In spring: firefly squid. Year-round: a sashimi set from any mid-range restaurant near the market will be better than most of what you'd eat in a Tokyo tourist district at twice the price.

Gold leaf and contemporary art

Kanazawa also has a serious craft tradition. Gold leaf production has been centered here since the Edo period 窶・99% of Japan's kinpaku (gold leaf) still comes from Kanazawa workshops. The '21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art' (designed by SANAA, open since 2004) is one of the better contemporary art museums in Japan and draws a local crowd, not just tourists.

Where to stay

Where to stay: the city center is small enough that almost anywhere is walkable. The area around Katamachi is convenient and has good restaurant density. Stay two nights minimum. The city rewards an unhurried second day in a way that a single overnight doesn't capture.

What Kanazawa has that Kyoto lost

What Kanazawa has that Kyoto has largely lost is the feeling of visiting somewhere people actually live. The geisha quarter isn't a theme park. The old town is genuinely old and genuinely in use. If you're looking for that 窶・and increasingly, it's what travelers say they want 窶・Kanazawa is close to the correct answer.
