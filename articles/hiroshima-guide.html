Hiroshima Is More Than the Peace Park — And the Peace Park Is More Than You Think

People visit Hiroshima for one reason. Most leave having found three or four others.

The Peace Memorial Park and Museum is why most people come to Hiroshima, and it should be. The museum documents what happened on August 6, 1945 with a directness and specificity that other memorial museums often avoid — personal objects, shadows burned into stone, recorded testimony. The Children's Peace Monument, the Genbaku Dome, the Flame of Peace that has burned continuously since 1964 and is meant to burn until all nuclear weapons are gone. Budget two to three hours, not one.

What you'll find beyond the memorial. Hiroshima has one of Japan's most interesting covered shotengai (shopping arcade) districts, centered around Hondori and its side streets. It's genuinely local — not a tourist market, but the arcade where people in Hiroshima buy clothes and eat lunch. The okonomiyaki restaurants here are a reason to visit Hiroshima independent of the memorial.

Hiroshima okonomiyaki is different from Osaka okonomiyaki. The Osaka version mixes everything together; the Hiroshima version layers ingredients — batter, bean sprouts, cabbage, noodles (yes, noodles), pork, egg — in sequence on a flat griddle. The result is denser and more substantial than the Osaka style. The specific place for it is Okonomimura, a three-floor building near the Hondori arcade with around 25 individual shops each making their version. Pick one that looks busy. Budget ¥1,000–1,400.

Miyajima Island: a 10-minute ferry from Hiroshima port (covered by the JR Pass). The vermilion torii gate of Itsukushima Shrine, standing in the sea at high tide, is one of those images that is on every Japan poster and still manages to be more striking in person than in photographs. Walk past the shrine to the hills — Daishoin Temple and the path up to Mt. Misen take you away from the day-trippers within 20 minutes. The island also has deer, which are smaller and less aggressive than Nara's but will absolutely eat your map.

Timing: Hiroshima is a half-day to full-day stop on the standard Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka route (Shinkansen from Shin-Osaka takes about 85 minutes). A full day allows the museum, Hondori, lunch, and a Miyajima trip. Staying overnight allows a more complete Miyajima experience and access to the city at night, when the riverfront around the Motoyasu-gawa is worth walking.
