Naoshima Is Japan's Art Island — and Nobody Tells You How to Do It Right

It's a real island with real people living on it. The art just happens to be everywhere.

What Naoshima is

Naoshima sits in the Seto Inland Sea between Okayama and Takamatsu, about an hour by ferry from either city. It's small enough to cross on a rented bicycle in an afternoon. The population is around 3,000. The island has more Monets per square kilometer than most museums in Europe, which is a fact that sounds invented but isn't.

How it became what it is

The backstory: in the 1980s and 90s, the Benesse Corporation commissioned architect Tadao Ando to build a museum on a largely depopulated island. What followed over the next three decades was a collaboration between art, architecture, and landscape that turned Naoshima into something that shouldn't quite work but absolutely does. It's been widely imitated. Nothing else is quite like it.

The Chichu Art Museum

The main venues. The Chichu Art Museum is underground — no exterior visible from above, all natural light coming through skylights — and contains exactly three artists: Monet (five late Water Lilies), James Turrell (light installations), and Walter De Maria. That's all. The ticket is ¥2,100 and it is worth the trip from Tokyo to stand in the Monet room, which is a thing I don't say lightly.

The Art House Project

The Art House Project is what separates Naoshima from every other art destination in Japan. Artists were given abandoned houses in Honmura village and told to do what they wanted. James Turrell built a light installation into a farmhouse that you enter barefoot and lie on the floor of. Hiroshi Sugimoto turned a shrine into something else. These aren't galleries with labels and benches. They're encounters.

Getting there

Getting there from Tokyo is a half-day commitment minimum. Bullet train to Okayama, 20 minutes by local train to Uno port, 20-minute ferry to Naoshima. From Takamatsu it's about an hour by ferry. The journey filters out casual visitors, which is exactly why the island still has the quality it has.

Stay overnight

Stay overnight if you can. After the day-trip visitors leave on the last ferry, the island changes. The Art House Project buildings glow from inside. The grounds around Benesse House are open and quiet. You'll be one of a small number of people who aren't locals. This is an unusual experience in Japan and should be used properly — walk around, eat at the small restaurant near the port, sit somewhere without your phone.

On bicycles

On bicycles: the island is hilly and summer heat is serious. Electric bikes are available at the ferry terminal and are not cheating. The climb between Honmura and the Benesse area in August on a regular bicycle is not a good time.

The right mindset

Naoshima rewards a particular mindset: willingness to be disoriented, to find things with no label, to sit with something that doesn't explain itself. If that's what you came to Japan for — and increasingly, it is — the island earns its reputation. If you're looking for informational plaques and a gift shop at every turn, the Benesse Foundation apologizes in advance.
