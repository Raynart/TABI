Meishi: The Japanese Business Card Ritual and Why It Matters

In Japan, the business card is not a piece of paper. It is a physical representation of the person. Treating it as stationary is the fastest way to start a professional relationship poorly.

In this article

  1. What a business card represents
  2. How to present your card
  3. How to receive a card
  4. What not to do
  5. If you don't have cards

What a business card represents

In Japan, the meishi (business card) exchange is a ritual that establishes who you are, your position, and your company 窶・in one gesture, at the start of every professional interaction. Cards are not handed out casually. They are exchanged at the beginning of a meeting with a formality that can feel significant to those not used to it. Understanding why makes the ritual easier: the card is standing in for a formal introduction that would otherwise take several minutes of verbal exchange.

How to present your card

Hold the card with both hands, text facing the recipient so they can read it immediately, and offer a slight bow as you present it. Say your name and company as you do. If you are handing out cards in a group, the most senior person goes first. Do not hand out cards across a table 窶・walk around or hand directly.

How to receive a card

Receive the card with both hands. Take a moment to read it 窶・not a cursory glance, but actually look at it. Acknowledge the person's name and company. If you are sitting down at a meeting, place the card on the table in front of you in order of seniority (most senior person's card nearest you, others in the order they were received). Do not put the card in your pocket or wallet until the meeting is over. The card on the table means the person is still present in the meeting, literally and symbolically.

What not to do

Do not write on a business card in front of the person who gave it to you. Do not fold it. Do not put it in your back pocket and sit on it. Do not place anything on top of it (not a cup, not another card). These actions read as treating the card 窶・and by extension the person 窶・carelessly. After the meeting, file cards appropriately. Japanese professionals use meishi holders specifically for this purpose.

If you don't have cards

Arriving at a Japanese business meeting without cards is not a catastrophe, but it is noticed. A brief explanation 窶・'I apologize, I have run out of cards' 窶・is appropriate. Most Japanese businesspeople will accept this gracefully. If you are planning any professional interactions in Japan, have cards printed. The Japanese side will often have English on the reverse, and printing the same for your own card is appreciated.
