Kyoto in Three Days: What to Actually See and What to Skip

Kyoto has over 1,600 temples and shrines. You will not see them all. Here is how to choose the ones that matter 窶・and what makes them worth choosing over the others.

In this article

  1. Day one: the east side
  2. Day one: evening in Gion
  3. Day two: the northwest
  4. Day two: Nishiki Market
  5. Day three: what most itineraries miss
  6. What to skip

Day one: the east side

Start at Fushimi Inari. The torii gate path that runs up Mt. Inari is the most photographed image in Japan, and the photographs don't exaggerate it. The path continues for four kilometers past the main tourist zone, thinning to almost nothing by the second kilometer. Arrive at 6:30am or earlier and walk past the first major platform 窶・the mountain path above it is genuinely quiet until 9am. In the afternoon: Kinkaku-ji (the Golden Pavilion). Arrive between 2pm and closing. It is always crowded; go anyway, because the building and its reflection in the pond are one of those things that maintains its impact despite the crowds. This is rarer than it sounds.

Day one: evening in Gion

After 5pm, walk through Gion. The most famous streets (Hanamikoji, Shimbashi) are genuinely beautiful in the evening light. In summer (July), the Gion Matsuri preparations fill the streets with yamahoko floats. In autumn, the maple trees along Shimbashi turn. Come to look, not to photograph 窶・or at minimum, look before you photograph. The neighborhood has been fighting the worst effects of mass tourism for several years, and the experience of being there is different when you are present rather than performing.

Day two: the northwest

Arashiyama is the west side's major district, accessible by the Sagano Scenic Railway or the Randen tram. The bamboo grove is a ten-minute walk from the Arashiyama station and genuinely spectacular 窶・and very crowded by 10am, nearly empty at 7am. After the grove: Tenryu-ji, one of Kyoto's great Zen gardens, with a pond framed by borrowed scenery (shakkei) of the mountains behind it. In autumn the maple trees around the pond are what the photographs in every Japan feature show. After Tenryu-ji, walk the Sagano path north to Jojakko-ji 窶・a moss-covered temple on a hillside that appears in almost no itineraries and is about as far from the crowd as you can get while staying in Arashiyama.

Day two: Nishiki Market

Return to central Kyoto in the afternoon for Nishiki Market 窶・a covered arcade called Kyoto's Kitchen, narrow, parallel to Shijo Street, with stalls selling pickled vegetables, grilled mochi, tofu, Kyoto-style dashi components, fresh yuba, and specialty food items. Eat as you walk (this is one of the exceptions to the no-eating-while-walking rule, since the market format makes stationary eating difficult). The market closes early 窶・most stalls done by 6pm.

Day three: what most itineraries miss

Philosopher's Path (Tetsugaku no Michi): a two-kilometer canal-side walkway between Ginkaku-ji and Nanzen-ji in the northeastern hills. In cherry blossom season the canal is lined with sakura. At any time of year it is quieter than the central tourist circuit and lined with small cafﾃｩs, tofu restaurants, and craft shops. Walk it slowly, south to north. At the northern end: Nanzen-ji, a Zen temple complex with an aqueduct running through the grounds 窶・one of the odder visual combinations in Kyoto and one of the most memorable.

What to skip

Nijo Castle: historically interesting, architecturally significant (the nightingale floor squeaks deliberately to detect intruders), and fairly dry as a visitor experience compared to everything else Kyoto offers. Come if you have four or more days. Toei Kyoto Studio Park (a movie set theme park): enthusiastically recommended by some and entirely optional for everyone who isn't specifically interested in period samurai dramas. Philosopher's Path during peak cherry blossom season: beautiful, but the crowd density removes the contemplative quality that is the entire point.
