Matsumoto: The Castle Town That Gets Everything Right

Two and a half hours from Tokyo, a city with one of Japan's three great original castles, a mountain backdrop, an excellent food scene, and a fraction of Kyoto's visitor numbers. There is no explanation for why more people don't come here.

In this article

  2. Inside the castle
  3. The old town
  4. Food and sake
  5. Getting there
  6. The Alps in winter

The castle

Matsumoto Castle is one of Japan's twelve surviving original castles 窶・meaning it was not destroyed, bombed, or rebuilt in concrete in the postwar era. The main tower (tenshu) dates to 1593. It is black, which is unusual (most Japanese castles are white), and it sits in a moat surrounded by mountains that the castle architects clearly intended as a backdrop. In spring there are cherry trees around the moat. In autumn there are maple trees. In winter there is snow on the Alps. In summer the castle is reflected in the moat in the early morning before the crowds arrive. Every season offers something specific. Entrance is ﾂ･700.

Inside the castle

The interior is original 窶・steep wooden staircases (more ladder than stair in the upper floors), low beams, the smell of old timber, defensive features like gun ports and stone-drop windows. It is small enough to feel like a real building rather than a museum installation, and large enough to spend an hour in. The views from the top floor extend across the city to the Alps. The interior can be crowded at peak times; arrive at 8:30am when it opens and you will often have the upper floors largely to yourself.

The old town

The Nakamachi and Kaichi districts preserve Edo-period kura (storehouses) that have been converted into craft shops, sake breweries, coffee shops, and restaurants. The streets are genuinely old and genuinely in use 窶・not a preserved historical theme zone but a working neighborhood that has been lived in continuously. The Kaichi School building (1876), built in a hybrid Japanese-Western style unique to Meiji-era architecture, is worth the five-minute detour.

Food and sake

Matsumoto is in Nagano Prefecture, which produces excellent sake, buckwheat, and mountain vegetables. The soba in Matsumoto is made from local buckwheat and is a different thing from the Tokyo variety 窶・coarser, darker, more robust. Mameichi, a small soba restaurant near the castle, is consistently recommended. The local sake breweries in Nakamachi offer tastings. Basho, a specialist sake bar near the station, has one of the better regional sake selections in the area.

Getting there

From Tokyo (Shinjuku): the Azusa Limited Express to Matsumoto takes 2h30m, costs around ﾂ･6,500 one-way, and is covered by JR Pass. From Nagoya: 2h by Shinano Limited Express. The city is small enough to walk everywhere once you arrive 窶・the castle is 15 minutes from the station on foot. A single full day is enough for the castle, old town, and a proper meal. Two days allows excursions to Kamikochi (30 minutes by bus, one of Japan's most beautiful alpine valleys) or Hotaka (20 minutes by train, a shrine with an unusual collection of religious art).

The Alps in winter

Matsumoto in winter 窶・December through February 窶・is exceptional if you are prepared for cold. The Alps are fully snowcapped. The castle moat reflects a stark black tower against white mountains on clear mornings. Visitor numbers are low. The onsen around the city (Asama, Utsukushigahara, Shirahone) are at their best in cold weather. Shirahone Onsen specifically, 90 minutes by bus from Matsumoto, is a small valley onsen village that has barely changed since the Edo period and is close to empty outside of August.
