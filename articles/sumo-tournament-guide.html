How to Watch a Sumo Tournament Without Watching It on TV

Six tournaments a year, three of them in Tokyo. Tickets go on sale months in advance and sell out. Here's how to actually get in.

In this article

  2. Getting tickets
  3. What you are watching
  4. When to arrive
  5. Food and drink at Kokugikan

Tournament schedule and cities

Sumo holds six official tournaments (basho) per year, each lasting 15 days. The January, May, and September basho are in Tokyo at Ryogoku Kokugikan. The March basho is in Osaka, July in Nagoya, and November in Fukuoka. If you're planning a trip around attending a tournament, knowing this schedule is step one. January and September in Tokyo are typically the most accessible for international visitors because they don't overlap with Golden Week or summer school holidays.

Getting tickets

Getting tickets: the Japan Sumo Association sells official tickets at Ticket Pia starting roughly two months before the tournament. The best seats (masu-seki, the box seats on the ground floor) sell out in days. What's available to visitors with short notice: elevated arena seats (isu-seki), which start around ﾂ･3,800 for the upper tiers, and same-day door tickets (ﾂ･2,200 for the cheap seats) that go on sale at the venue from 8am. The door ticket strategy works 窶・arrive at Ryogoku at 7:30am and you will get in.

What you are watching

What you're watching: sumo matches are short. The actual bout can last anywhere from two seconds to two minutes. The bulk of the time in a sumo tournament is ritual 窶・the ring-entering ceremony (dohyo-iri) for the highest-ranked wrestlers, salt throwing, the slow psychological confrontation before the wrestlers crouch and charge. Understanding that the ceremony is the event, not the prelude to it, changes everything about how to watch.

When to arrive

Timing: the day begins at 8am with the lowest-ranked wrestlers, and the audience trickles in as the day progresses. The top-division matches (makuuchi) begin around 3pm and end around 6pm. Most visitors arrive around 2pm, which is correct. The atmosphere builds through the afternoon. The final few bouts of the day 窶・particularly if a yokozuna or ozeki is involved in a high-stakes match 窶・generate a noise level that is genuinely startling.

Food and drink at Kokugikan

What to eat and drink at the venue: the basement of Kokugikan has chankonabe, the stew traditionally eaten by sumo wrestlers. It is not a gimmick. It's good stew, with chicken and vegetables in a clear dashi broth, and it costs around ﾂ･1,000. There are also yakitori stalls and beer vendors. Food at the masu boxes is served in bento-style sets and can be ordered in advance with tickets.

What surprises you in person

The wrestlers are larger than you expect. The ring is smaller than you expect. The speed of the bouts is faster than you expect. These three surprises reliably improve the live experience over whatever you were imagining from television coverage.
